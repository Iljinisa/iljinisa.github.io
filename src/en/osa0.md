The course is an introduction to _software production_ for those who already master programming, i.e. a systematic way of making slightly larger software in a team of several people for an external customer. The course has a special emphasis on so-called agile software production methods.

After completing the course unit, you understand the terminology related to the software life cycle and know how to apply methodological principles and practices of software production (e.g. Scrum) in your work. More specifically, you

- know the phases of software production, especially agile software production,
- know how requirements are managed in agile software production,
- understand the responsibilities and nature of design, implementation, and testing in agile software production,
- understand the basics of software quality management,
- identify the economic constraints of software development, and
- know how to act in an environment where software development takes place in a controlled and repeatable manner.

## Course completion and grading

The course consists of three components: lectures, calculators, and a mini-project.

### Lectures

The course has 11 lectures and several guest lectures. The lectures mainly go through terminology and theory related to software production, the same thing this material focuses on.

### Assignments

The course includes weekly calculations. There are two types of assignments.

The theory covered in the lectures and in this material is reviewed in the _weekly_ tasks (multiple-choice or short reflection tasks), the deadline for which is Sunday at 11:59 PM.
The second part of the tasks deals with more technical things related to software production, such as version control, testing, and software configuration, their deadline is _Monday at 11:59 PM_. The programming language for the tasks is Python.

The expected workload of the calculations is more during the first three weeks and less after that. Answering theory questions is relatively quick, but answering them sensibly requires participating in lectures and/or reading the week's material.

### Mini-project

Towards the end of the course, _a mini-project_ is organized, i.e., a small group work, the main purpose of which is to practice project management and certain quality management techniques.

Each mini-project group consists of 4-6 students, and the groups also have a customer whom the group meets weekly. In the first week, 60 minutes should be reserved for the customer meeting, and 30 minutes in the following weeks.

At the end of the course, there is a joint demo session for the mini-projects.

You should reserve a total of about 6 hours per week for working on mini-projects.

_The prerequisite for passing the course_ is a successfully completed or credited mini-project.

Mini-projects meet the customer on campus. One or two remote groups will be formed if necessary.

#### Recognition of prior learning for the mini-project

Participating in the mini-project is not necessary if you have at least _three months of work experience_ working in a software development team. If you credit the mini-project with work experience, let us know by email (petri.j.ihantola@jyu.fi) at the stage when you have done the first tasks (in week 2). For accredited projects, a panel discussion will be organized for one lecture session (the date will be agreed upon together with the students), where students who have gained work experience can share their experiences. The course teacher will moderate the panel discussion.

### Course grading

Points are awarded for different areas of the course. The weights of the areas in the final assessment are:

- Multiple-choice tasks 10%
- Configuration tasks 15%
- Mini-project 25% points
- Exam 45%
- Participation in guest lectures 5%


Passing requires a successfully completed mini-project (or recognition of prior learning) and a successfully completed exam.

### Lectures - calculators - mini-project

The course thus includes three main components: lectures, weekly calculations, and a mini-project. The components are quite different in nature, and this has sometimes caused confusion among students.

The course lectures mainly focus on software development theory and terminology. Of the calculations, the multiple-choice questions relate to each week's lectures.

The topics of the more technical tasks dealing with version control, software configuration, testing, and programming are not actually addressed in the lectures at all (except for the theory related to testing).

The idea of the mini-project is to combine the theory from the lectures and the more technical things covered in the calculations and apply them in practice in a small software project.

In the exam, the greatest weight will be on theory and its application to practice. The most technical aspects of the calculations, such as version control, will not be asked in the exam. More details about the exam and preparing for it will be provided in the last lecture of the course.

As we will soon see, software production covers a wide range of things, from people management to the most technical operations performed from the command line. The same is reflected in the structure of the course; the course has different components, none of which is more important than the others, each having its own weight in the course grading. Theory matters are assessed based on exam performance, and more practical matters as a "continuous assessment" based on the points accumulated from the calculations and performance in the mini-project.
