The course is an introduction *to software production* for those who already know how to program, i.e., a systematic way of making slightly more extensive software in a team of several people for an external customer. The course places particular emphasis on so-called agile software production methods.

After completing the course, you will understand the concepts related to the software life cycle and be able to apply the methodological principles and practices of software production (e.g., Scrum) in your work. More specifically

- you will be familiar with the stages of software production, especially agile software production
- you will know how requirements are managed in agile software production,
- you will understand the responsibilities and nature of design, implementation, and testing in agile software production,
- you understand the basics of software quality management,
- you recognize the economic constraints of software development, and
- you will be able to work in an environment where software development is carried out in a controlled and repeatable manner.

## Course completion and assessment

The course consists of three components: lectures, calculation exercises, and a mini-project.

### Lectures

The course includes 11 lectures and several guest lectures. The lectures mainly cover concepts and theory related to software production, which is the focus of this material.

### Calculation exercises

The course includes weekly calculation exercises. There are two types of exercises.

The deadline for *weekly* assignments (multiple choice or short reflection questions) that review the theory covered in the lectures and this material is Sunday at 11:59 p.m. The other part of the assignments deals with more technical issues related to software production, such as version control, testing, and software configuration. The deadline for these is *Monday at 11:59 p.m*. The programming language for the assignments is Python.

The expected workload for the calculation exercises will be higher during the first three weeks and lower thereafter. The theory assignments can be completed relatively quickly, but in order to answer them sensibly, you will need to attend lectures and/or read the weekly material.

### Mini-project

Towards the end of the course, there will be *a mini-project*, i.e., a small group assignment, the main purpose of which is to practice project management and certain quality management techniques.

Each mini-project group consists of 4-6 students, and the groups also have a client whom they meet with weekly. In the first week, 60 minutes should be reserved for the client meeting, and 30 minutes in the following weeks.

At the end of the course, there will be a joint demo session for the mini-projects.

A total of approximately 6 hours per week should be reserved for working on the mini-projects.

A successfully completed or approved mini-project is *a prerequisite for passing the course*.

The mini-projects will meet with the client on campus. If necessary, one or two remote groups will be formed.

#### Credit transfer for mini-projects

Participation in the mini-project is not necessary if you have at least *three months* of *work experience* in a software development team. If you are credited for the mini-project based on your work experience, please notify us by email (petri.j.ihantola@jyu.fi) once you have completed the first assignments (in week 2). A panel discussion will be organized for approved projects (the date will be agreed upon with the students) during one lecture, where students with work experience can share their experiences. The course instructor will moderate the panel discussion.

### Course assessment

Points are awarded for different parts of the course. The weightings of the different parts in the final assessment are as follows:

- Multiple-choice questions 10%
- Configuration assignments 15%
- Mini-project 25% of points
- exam 45%
- Participation in guest lectures 5%

Passing the course requires successful completion (or credit) of the mini-project and passing the exam.

### Lectures - calculation exercises - mini-project

The course therefore consists of three main components: lectures, weekly calculation exercises, and a mini-project. These components are quite different in nature, which has sometimes caused confusion among students.

The lectures mainly focus on the theory and concepts of software development. The multiple-choice questions relate to each week\'s lectures.

More technical topics such as version control, software configuration, testing, and programming are not really covered in the lectures at all (except for the theory related to testing).

The idea behind the mini-project is to combine the theory from the lectures with the more technical topics covered in the exercises and apply them in practice in a small software project.

The exam will focus on theory and its practical application. The more technical aspects covered in the tutorials, such as version control, will not be included in the exam. More details about the exam and how to prepare for it will be provided in the last lecture of the course.

As we will soon see, software production covers a wide range of topics, from managing people to more technical command line operations. This is also reflected in the structure of the course, which has different components, none of which is more important than the others; each has its own weight in the course assessment. Theoretical matters are assessed on the basis of exam results, while more practical matters are assessed on the basis of a \"continuous assessment\" of points accumulated from quizzes and performance in mini-projects.
